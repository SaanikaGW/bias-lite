Tips & Tricks to Avoid Gender Bias in Writing

Gender bias in writing subtly reinforces stereotypes that limit people and shape how readers judge intelligence, leadership, and capability. Biased language can make texts feel exclusionary or outdated, while neutral, inclusive wording improves fairness and credibility.

Common Forms of Gender Bias

  • Generic masculines: Using “he” or “mankind” for everyone implies maleness as default.
  • Gendered job titles: policeman, stewardess, chairman frame roles as gender-specific.
  • Stereotyped descriptions: Women as “emotional/supportive,” men as “logical/strong.”
  • Order bias: Repeating one order (e.g., “men and women”) can imply hierarchy.

How to Avoid It

  • Use neutral titles: firefighter, chairperson, server, spokesperson.
  • Prefer they/them or plural phrasing to avoid forced pronouns.
  • Focus on roles and actions, not gendered traits.
  • Check adjectives—describe professional competence, not appearance or emotion.
  • Balance representation in examples and imagery across roles and contexts.

Why It Matters

Language normalizes expectations. In academic and professional contexts, biased wording can distort interpretation, reduce trust, and alienate audiences. Inclusive language is accurate, respectful, and effective.

Example and Mitigation

  • Biased: The businessman must present his ideas clearly.
  • Inclusive: Business professionals must present their ideas clearly.

Unbiased language is not just ethical—it is clear communication that reflects modern awareness.
